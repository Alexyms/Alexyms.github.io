NYC Taxi Network Vulnerability Analysis

Applied percolation theory from statistical physics to 3 million taxi trips across 263 NYC zones. The network doesn't break with a dramatic phase transition. It's deeply unequal from the start, and that inequality follows borough lines with striking precision.

3M Trips Analyzed
263 Taxi Zones
12 Time Windows
5 Boroughs

The Question

New York City's Taxi and Limousine Commission divides the five boroughs into 263 taxi zones. Every time a yellow or green taxi picks up a passenger in one zone and drops them in another, it traces an edge in a transportation network. A route with hundreds of trips per day is a robust link. A route with one or two trips per month is barely a whisper.

The question is deceptively simple: does this network serve all New Yorkers equally? And when you raise the bar on what counts as 'connected,' who gets disconnected first?

The Approach

This analysis borrows from percolation theory, a branch of statistical physics that studies how networks fall apart. The classic example is water flowing through a porous rock: if enough of the tiny channels are open, water can find a path from top to bottom. Block enough of them, and the flow stops. The point where the network goes from 'connected' to 'broken' is called the percolation threshold, and in many physical systems it happens suddenly, like a dam breaking.

The same framework applies to any network. In this case, each taxi zone is a node, and a trip from one zone to another creates a directed edge between them. 'Directed' means direction matters: 100 taxis going from Manhattan to Brooklyn doesn't mean 100 go the other way.

The key tool is a threshold called theta. Set theta to 5, and an edge only exists between two zones if at least 5 trips per month travel that route. Set it to 50, and only the busiest corridors survive. By slowly raising theta and watching what happens to the network, you can measure exactly how resilient the system is and where it breaks first.

The main metric is the Giant Strongly Connected Component (Giant SCC): the largest group of zones where you can get from any zone to any other zone through some chain of taxi trips, and back again. When the Giant SCC covers most of the network, the system is healthy. When it shrinks, zones are becoming islands. When it vanishes for a borough, that borough is effectively cut off from the taxi network.

The hypothesis going in was that there would be a clear percolation threshold: some value of theta where the network suddenly shatters, the way classical theory predicts. That would tell policymakers exactly what minimum service level the system needs to maintain connectivity. The dataset covers all yellow and green taxi trips in January 2024, approximately 3 million trips across 12 two-hour time windows spanning each day. This gives both spatial and temporal resolution: not just where the network is weak, but when.

What I Expected vs. What I Found

I expected textbook percolation behavior: a sharp phase transition. In classical percolation theory, there's usually a critical threshold where the network abruptly shatters. One moment it's mostly connected, the next it's in fragments.

That's not what happened. The NYC taxi network begins fragmenting almost immediately. The median edge weight across all zone pairs is just 2 trips per month. Most connections are gossamer-thin, and they vanish the moment you demand even modest service levels. There is no dramatic cliff, just a steady erosion of connectivity that begins at the very first threshold.

This means the original question, 'at what threshold does the network break?', turned out to be the wrong question. The network was never well-connected enough to break dramatically. Instead, the more informative framing is differential vulnerability: how does each borough's connectivity degrade as the service standard increases, and who loses access first?

Borough Inequality

The answer falls along borough lines with almost mathematical precision. At a threshold of theta = 10 trips per month (roughly one trip every three days), Manhattan retains 82% of its zones in the connected core. Queens holds on at 18%, buoyed largely by JFK Airport traffic. Brooklyn drops to just 4%. The Bronx falls to 0%. Staten Island was never meaningfully connected to begin with.

These aren't cherry-picked numbers. At every threshold tested, the same hierarchy holds. Even at theta = 20, Manhattan still maintains 75% connectivity while every other borough has effectively collapsed.

Why does the Bronx fare so poorly? It comes down to infrastructure: only 3 Manhattan zones serve as 'bridge zones' connecting to the Bronx, compared to 60 for Queens and 11 for Brooklyn. With so few connectors, losing even a handful of low-traffic edges severs the Bronx entirely. And when outer borough zones lose their connection to Manhattan, they don't form local clusters. There are too few intra-borough taxi trips to sustain local networks. The Giant SCC is essentially 'the Manhattan cluster plus whoever can still reach Manhattan and back.'

The Taxi Home Effect

Perhaps the most striking finding is a massive directional asymmetry in evening taxi flows. During morning hours, taxi flows are relatively balanced and multi-directional. But come evening, the pattern transforms: flows become overwhelmingly one-directional, pouring out of Manhattan into the outer boroughs. Brooklyn sees Manhattan-to-Brooklyn trips outnumber the reverse at a ratio of up to 192 to 1.

New Yorkers in the outer boroughs commute to Manhattan by subway and bus in the morning, but when the workday ends, or after dinner, or after a show, they take a cab back. The taxi network isn't competing with transit for the morning commute. It's filling a different role entirely, serving as return transportation for the evening.

This has a cascading effect on morning connectivity. Taxis accumulate in outer boroughs overnight, following their evening passengers. But the next morning's demand originates in Manhattan, creating a spatial mismatch that likely contributes to the outer boroughs' weaker morning service.

Development Approach

This project also served as a learning exercise in AI-assisted development. The analysis pipeline was built using a six-agent orchestration system in Claude Code, with specialized agents for planning, data processing, network analysis, visualization, testing, and code review. Each agent had a defined role and structured handoff protocols, enabling parallel development of independent pipeline stages while maintaining quality control through review cycles. The full pipeline was validated for reproducibility by running end-to-end on a second machine.

What Comes Next

This analysis covers a single month of data. Weather integration is the most natural extension: do rain and snow events accelerate fragmentation? A multi-month comparison would reveal seasonal patterns. And ride-share data from Uber and Lyft could reveal whether these services fill the gaps identified here or reinforce the same Manhattan-centric patterns. The TLC publishes ride-share trip records in the same format, making direct comparison feasible.
